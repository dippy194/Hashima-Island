Hashima Island

The Dark Past of Japan's Battleship Island

History

Hashima Island, also known as Gunkanjima ("Battleship Island"), was once a thriving coal mining facility run by Mitsubishi from 1887 to 1974. In the early 20th century, the island became a symbol of rapid industrialization in Japan, with concrete buildings, a dense population, and modern amenities for workers.

"It was the future of Japan — stacked in concrete." — Former Resident

Korean Forced Labor

During WWII, hundreds of Koreans were forced to work in inhumane conditions on Hashima Island. According to survivors and records, workers endured long hours, poor nutrition, and abuse. This legacy remains controversial in East Asian memory politics.

"We were treated like machines, not humans." — Korean survivor testimony via Yonhap News

UNESCO Controversy

In 2015, Japan succeeded in having Hashima Island registered as a UNESCO World Heritage site for its contribution to the Meiji Industrial Revolution. However, Korea and other nations objected to the omission of forced labor in Japan’s narrative. Although Japan promised to acknowledge the full history, critics argue that efforts have fallen short.

"Japan has a responsibility to present the full history — not just the parts it’s proud of." — JNTO Historian

Timeline

1887

Start of Operations

Mitsubishi begins coal mining on Hashima Island, establishing its role in Japan's industrial revolution.

1916

Concrete Infrastructure

The first large concrete building in Japan is built on Hashima to withstand typhoons and house miners.

1940s

Forced Labor Era

During WWII, Korean and Chinese laborers are brought to the island under brutal, coercive conditions.

1959

Population Peak

Hashima Island reaches its peak population with over 5,000 people crammed into the small space.

1974

Closure and Abandonment

Coal reserves run out; Mitsubishi closes the mine. The island is abandoned overnight.

2009

Reopening for Tourism

Hashima is reopened to the public with limited tourist access to preserve safety and history.

2015

UNESCO World Heritage

Despite controversy, the island is designated as a World Heritage Site, highlighting Japan's modernization.

2020s

Ongoing Debate

Japan faces criticism for not fully addressing the forced labor history in its official UNESCO display.
