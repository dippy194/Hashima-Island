Hashima Island

A Dark Chapter in Industrial History

History of Hashima

Hashima Island, also known as Gunkanjima ("Battleship Island"), was a coal mining facility operated by Mitsubishi since 1890. It was a symbol of Japan’s rapid industrialization and featured the country’s first large concrete apartment building built in 1916 to resist typhoons.

By 1959, the island housed over 5,000 people, making it one of the most densely populated places on Earth at the time. Despite its small size, it was a complete city with a school, hospital, shops, and recreational areas.

Forced Labor During WWII

During Japan’s colonization of Korea and parts of China, laborers were conscripted to Hashima Island under brutal conditions. Korean and Chinese workers were forced to work in dangerous mines, enduring long hours, physical abuse, and lack of freedom.

“It was like living in hell. You never knew if you would come back alive from the mines.” — Korean survivor testimony

“I was only a teenager. We were constantly watched and beaten. We never saw daylight.” — Survivor quoted by Yonhap News

Despite postwar reparations and partial acknowledgments, the full history of the forced laborers remains controversial and contested in international discussions.

Historical Timeline

1890: Mitsubishi purchases the island and begins mining operations.

1916: Japan's first large concrete apartment block is constructed on the island.

1930s-40s: During WWII, forced labor of Korean and Chinese individuals begins.

1945: Japan surrenders; island continues to operate post-war.

1959: Population reaches peak with over 5,200 residents.

1974: Coal reserves decline; mine closes and island is abandoned.

2009: Tours to the island begin.

2015: Hashima is designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site, sparking debate over labor history.
