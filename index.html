Hashima Island

Industry, Abandonment, and the Shadow of History

Introduction

Hashima Island (端島), commonly called Gunkanjima or "Battleship Island," lies off the coast of Nagasaki, Japan. The nickname derives from its warship-like appearance due to its concrete sea walls and dense architecture. Though initially known for its coal reserves and rapid industrial growth, the island also bears scars from Japan's darker wartime past.

As Japan National Tourism Organization describes, the island symbolizes Japan’s industrial revolution. However, as time passed, survivors and researchers uncovered forced labor operations that had been buried beneath the industrial pride.

Industrial Rise: The Mitsubishi Era

Mitsubishi acquired the island in 1890 to exploit its rich coal reserves. By digging deep under the ocean floor, it engineered one of the most technically advanced undersea mining systems of its time. In 1916, Japan's first reinforced concrete apartment block was built here, setting the stage for vertical urban life.

At its peak, Hashima held over 5,000 people within a land area of just 6.3 hectares — a population density that was nine times higher than Tokyo’s.

“At its peak, more than 5,000 people lived on this small, 6.3-hectare island.” — Japan National Tourism Organization

Life on the Island

The workers and their families lived in stacked apartment blocks with limited space. The island featured a school, a cinema, communal baths, and even rooftop playgrounds due to space constraints. Food, water, and supplies were imported from the mainland daily. The confined, concrete environment made life both innovative and claustrophobic.

Forced Korean Labor During WWII

From 1939 to 1945, the Japanese Empire brought more than 800 Koreans to Hashima to work under coercive conditions. These laborers were often unpaid, overworked, and physically abused. Yonhap News reported that survivors likened the island to a prison where escape was impossible.

“We were forced to work underground for more than 12 hours a day… many were injured, and some even died.” — Yonhap News Agency

Decline and Abandonment

As petroleum replaced coal in the 1960s, Japan’s dependence on mining dwindled. Hashima’s mines closed in 1974, and residents were forced to evacuate rapidly. The island was left intact — beds, dishes, and chalkboards still in place, giving the site a hauntingly preserved atmosphere.

UNESCO World Heritage Status and Controversy

In 2015, Hashima Island was added to the UNESCO World Heritage list. However, many — especially from South Korea — criticized the Japanese government for failing to clearly acknowledge the use of forced labor on the island. While Japan promised to honor these stories, signage remains vague, and advocacy groups continue pushing for clearer memorialization.
