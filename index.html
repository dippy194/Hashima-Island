Hashima Island

A Dark Chapter in Industrial History

History of Hashima

Hashima Island, also known as Gunkanjima ("Battleship Island"), was once a thriving coal mining facility operated by Mitsubishi since 1890. It became a symbol of Japan's rapid industrialization in the early 20th century.

The island had Japan’s first large concrete building, constructed in 1916 to protect against typhoons. At its peak in 1959, over 5,000 people lived on the tiny island, making it one of the most densely populated places on Earth.

Forced Labor During WWII

During Japan’s occupation of Korea and parts of China in WWII, forced labor was used on Hashima Island. Many Korean and Chinese laborers were brought to the island under brutal conditions.

“They treated us like animals. We were not allowed to leave, and the conditions underground were horrific.” — Korean survivor, quoted by Yonhap News

The laborers faced hazardous mining conditions, long hours, and abuse. Despite Mitsubishi and Japan acknowledging the island’s historical role, the extent of abuse remains a subject of dispute.

Historical Timeline

1890: Mitsubishi buys the island and begins coal mining.

1916: Japan's first large concrete apartment building built.

1940s: Forced Korean and Chinese laborers brought to island.

1959: Population peaks at over 5,000 people.

1974: Mine closes; island is abandoned.

2015: Hashima becomes UNESCO World Heritage Site.
