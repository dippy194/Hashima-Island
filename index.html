Hashima Island

Industry, Abandonment, and the Shadow of History

History of Hashima

Hashima Island, also known as Gunkanjima or "Battleship Island," was purchased by Mitsubishi in 1890 to support Japan's rapidly industrializing economy. Coal mining began shortly after and grew into a massive operation, with tall concrete buildings and a self-sustaining community built on the tiny island.

"Gunkanjima was a symbol of Japan’s industrial pride—until it became a ghost island." – Japan National Tourism Organization

Korean Forced Labor

During World War II, the Japanese government forcibly conscripted Korean civilians to work in harsh conditions on Hashima Island. They were subjected to dangerous labor, inadequate food, and constant surveillance. The suffering of these workers remains a point of contention in East Asian politics today.

"They beat us for not working fast enough. We were just kids." – Korean Survivor Testimony, Yonhap News

Abandonment

With the decline of coal in favor of petroleum, operations on the island ceased in 1974. The island was completely abandoned, leaving behind towering ruins and a haunting silence. Nature began to reclaim the site, but the damage of war and exploitation remains etched into its decaying structures.

Timeline

1890: Mitsubishi buys Hashima for coal mining.
1916: First large concrete building is built on the island.
1940s: Korean and Chinese forced laborers brought to the island.
1974: Coal reserves depleted; island abandoned.
2009: UNESCO considers adding Hashima to World Heritage Sites.
2015: Site designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, stirring international controversy.

Legacy and Controversy

Despite being recognized for its historical value, Hashima remains a symbol of pain for many. UNESCO's recognition required Japan to acknowledge the forced labor that took place, but some survivors and activists argue that the island's full history has not been adequately shared.

"Recognition without remembrance is a hollow gesture." – Human Rights Watch
