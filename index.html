Hashima Island

Overview

Hashima Island, also known as Gunkanjima or "Battleship Island", is a tiny abandoned island located off the coast of Nagasaki, Japan. Once a thriving coal mining facility, it became a symbol of Japan’s rapid industrialization. The island was later abandoned in 1974 when coal reserves depleted.

Historical Significance

The Mitsubishi Corporation purchased the island in 1890 and developed it into a coal mining facility. At its peak in 1959, over 5,000 residents lived on the island, making it one of the most densely populated places in the world at the time.

"It was the land of no return." — Former Korean forced laborer

Forced Labor During WWII

During World War II, Korean and Chinese laborers were forcibly brought to Hashima to work under extremely harsh conditions. Many survivors have shared accounts of the brutal treatment and inhumane conditions they endured.

“We were treated worse than animals.” — Korean survivor interviewed by Yonhap News

Modern Recognition

In 2015, Hashima was recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage site as part of Japan’s “Sites of Japan’s Meiji Industrial Revolution.” However, the designation was met with controversy due to Japan’s failure to fully acknowledge the forced labor history.

“Acknowledging history is the first step toward healing.” — UNESCO Representative

Timeline

1890 Mitsubishi purchases Hashima and begins mining operations.
1916 Japan’s first large reinforced concrete building constructed on the island.
1940s Korean and Chinese forced laborers brought during World War II.
1959 Population peaks at 5,259, one of the highest densities in the world.
1974 Mitsubishi closes the mine and the island is abandoned.
2009 Hashima reopens to tourism with limited access.
2015 Designated a UNESCO World Heritage site.
