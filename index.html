Korean Forced Labor

During WWII, Korean civilians were forcibly conscripted to work on Hashima Island. These laborers faced harsh conditions: grueling shifts, underground heat, little food, and physical abuse. Many survivors have called for recognition and memorialization of their suffering.

"They beat us for not working fast enough. We were just kids." ‚Äì Korean Survivor, Yonhap News

Abandonment

As Japan shifted to oil in the 1970s, coal demand plummeted. In 1974, Mitsubishi shut down the mine and evacuated the island. Buildings, once full of life, were left to deteriorate. The island became a symbol of forgotten industrial ambition.

Timeline

  • 1890: Mitsubishi purchases the island and begins mining.
  • 1916: Japan's first large concrete building is built.
  • 1940s: Korean and Chinese forced laborers arrive.
  • 1959: Island population peaks at over 5,000 people.
  • 1974: Mine closes; island is abandoned.
  • 2009: Public tours begin for limited visitors.
  • 2015: Recognized as a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Legacy and Controversy

Hashima's recognition as a UNESCO site stirred debate. South Korea and others criticized Japan for not fully acknowledging the suffering of forced laborers. Although a condition of the designation was to share the full story, critics say this has not been meaningfully upheld.
