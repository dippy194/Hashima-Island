Hashima Island – The Lost Ghost Island of Japan

By Lucas Aladag

Introduction

If you’ve ever seen photos of a giant, abandoned concrete island off the coast of Japan, you’re probably looking at Hashima Island—sometimes called Gunkanjima or “Battleship Island.” It’s about 15 kilometers from Nagasaki, and the whole place is basically a cluster of empty apartment blocks and factories rising out of the sea. What’s wild is that this used to be a packed mining community with over 5,000 people, and now it’s totally deserted. I wanted to dig into what made Hashima so important and why it’s not just another ghost town (Japan Travel).

Industrial Growth and Peak

Hashima’s story actually started back in the early 1800s when coal was found there, but things only really kicked off when Mitsubishi bought the island in 1890. They turned it into a hardcore coal mining base. Whole families lived in these concrete buildings built right on the edge of the island, and almost every bit of space was used. By the 1950s and 60s, it was known for having the highest population density in the world. People basically lived, worked, and went to school in these apartment blocks, and everything they needed—from shops to a hospital—was squeezed onto the island. It sounds kind of cool at first, but it was honestly pretty intense, and not everyone was there by choice (Japan Travel).

Life on the Island

Daily life on Hashima was kind of a mix of community and survival. Residents say everyone knew each other, and there were even schools and playgrounds for the kids. The company set up communal baths, movie screenings, and small shops. But it wasn’t all fun—there were typhoons, cramped apartments, and basically no escape except by boat. Fresh water had to be shipped in every day, and everything was run by the mining company. Still, a lot of people look back on life there as a unique experience—some say it was like living in a “floating city” out in the middle of the ocean (Japan Travel).

Korean and Chinese Forced Labor

If there’s one part of Hashima’s story that really stands out—and not in a good way—it’s what went on during World War II. Back then, Japan was desperate for coal, so they started bringing in workers from Korea and China and forcing them to dig in places like Hashima. From what I read, the conditions were brutal. People had to work crazy long hours, sometimes in really dangerous tunnels, for almost no money. It wasn’t just about the hard work—there was barely enough food, a lot of the guys got sick or injured, and the way they were treated was just terrible. They basically had to live in these crowded barracks and couldn’t do anything about it. For a long time, nobody really talked about what happened to them, but I think their side of the story matters a lot. You can’t understand Hashima without knowing what those people went through (Yonhap News).

Abandonment and Modern Rediscovery

When Japan switched from coal to oil in the 1970s, there was suddenly no reason to keep the mine open. Almost overnight, Mitsubishi shut everything down and told everyone to leave. People pretty much grabbed what they could and left most of their stuff behind, so the island looks like time just stopped. For years, Hashima was off-limits, but in the 2000s, it opened up for tours and started showing up in movies and video games. Even the James Bond movie "Skyfall" used it as inspiration for the villain’s hideout (Japan Travel).

Preservation, Tourism & Pop Culture

Nowadays, a lot of the island is in rough shape—some buildings are barely standing, and there are safety rails everywhere to keep tourists from getting hurt. The Japanese government and UNESCO have tried to protect what’s left, but it’s not easy. Hashima has become a symbol of both Japan’s industrial success and the darker stuff people would rather forget. It keeps popping up in documentaries and pop culture, which kind of forces everyone to talk about the full story, not just the cool ruins (Japan Travel).

Why Hashima Island Should Be Taught in Schools

If you ask me why Hashima Island should be taught in schools, it’s because it’s a real-life example of what happens when progress comes with a price. Usually, when we learn about history, it’s all about inventions, wars, or leaders, but Hashima tells the story of the regular people caught in between. It’s not just about the past either—it’s about choices people make today and what those choices cost.

Learning about Hashima means being honest about the fact that history isn’t always as simple as it looks. The stories of the Korean and Chinese laborers, especially, show how easy it is for people’s suffering to get ignored when everyone just wants to talk about “success.” In my opinion, facing that part of history, no matter how uncomfortable it is, helps us understand why telling the truth matters.

It also gets you thinking about things like the environment, workers’ rights, and how cities and communities get built—or torn apart—by big companies or governments. By having Hashima in the curriculum, students can think about what kind of world they want to live in, not just memorize dates. That’s kind of the whole point of school, right?

Timeline of Hashima Island

1810

  • Coal is discovered on Hashima Island for the first time.
  • The island is mostly uninhabited, but now it’s on Japan’s radar.

1890

  • Mitsubishi buys Hashima and starts building a massive mining operation.
  • First concrete apartment blocks go up for workers and their families.

1930s-1945

  • Japan gears up for war and the demand for coal skyrockets.
  • Korean and Chinese laborers are forced to work in dangerous conditions.
  • The island’s population grows fast and life gets even tougher.

1950s-1960s

  • Peak years for Hashima—over 5,000 people live there.
  • The island is famous for being the most crowded place in the world.
  • Kids go to school, families shop and go to movies on the island.

1974

  • Mitsubishi closes the mine after coal demand collapses.
  • Everyone leaves the island, and Hashima is totally abandoned.

2009-present

  • Hashima reopens for tours and is named a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
  • People from all over the world visit to learn about its history and see the ruins.
