Hashima Island

Introduction

Hashima Island, also known as Gunkanjima or "Battleship Island," is a tiny abandoned island off the coast of Nagasaki, Japan. It rose to global fame for its eerie ruins and dark history, becoming a symbol of rapid industrialization and the human cost behind it.

“The coal was our life, but it came at a cost none of us could forget.” — Korean Survivor Testimony

History and Industrial Use

From 1887 to 1974, Mitsubishi operated a massive undersea coal mine here. The island became one of the most densely populated places on Earth, housing over 5,000 residents. Its apartment complexes, schools, hospital, and market all existed on the less-than-one-square-kilometer landmass.

"We lived stacked on top of each other, like coal in a shaft." — Former Resident

Use of Forced Labor

During World War II, Korean and Chinese laborers were forcibly brought to Hashima to work in brutal conditions. According to JNTO, thousands worked in the mines, often without sufficient food or rest. Yonhap News reports that many died underground or from disease and injuries.

“Even sunlight couldn’t reach us in those tunnels. We forgot what day it was.” — Korean Laborer Account

Timeline

1887

Mitsubishi begins coal mining operations on the island.

1916

Japan's first large reinforced concrete building is constructed on Hashima.

1940s

Forced labor of Koreans and Chinese reaches peak during WWII.

1959

Population reaches over 5,200 people—densest on Earth.

1974

Coal reserves run dry. The mine closes and Hashima is abandoned.

2015

Hashima is designated as a UNESCO World Heritage Site, sparking debate over labor history acknowledgment.
