Hashima Island

From Industrial Might to Historical Reckoning

Welcome to Hashima

Hashima Island, also known as Gunkanjima or "Battleship Island," is a haunting relic of Japan’s industrial past. Located near Nagasaki, this once-thriving coal mining facility now stands abandoned — a concrete shell with a layered history.

Industrial Boom

In 1890, Mitsubishi purchased Hashima and transformed it into a coal mining powerhouse. According to the Japan National Tourism Organization, “At its peak, more than 5,000 people lived on this small, 6.3-hectare island.”

“Mitsubishi developed the island to extract coal from undersea mines, and people lived in concrete buildings with rooftop gardens and shops.” — JNTO

Forced Labor: The Untold Chapter

During World War II, Hashima’s operations relied heavily on forced labor — particularly from Korea. Workers endured extreme conditions in the mines. As reported by Yonhap News:

“We were forced to work underground for more than 12 hours a day... many were injured, and some even died.” — Yonhap News

Despite international criticism, the Japanese government has been reluctant to fully acknowledge this chapter.

Today’s Debate

The image of Hashima Island in media and tourism is often focused on its dramatic architecture. But many believe this overlooks the suffering endured there. UNESCO has encouraged Japan to present “the full history” of such sites.
