Hashima Island

Introduction

Hashima Island, also known as Gunkanjima or "Battleship Island," is a small abandoned island located off the coast of Nagasaki, Japan. It was once a thriving coal mining facility operated by Mitsubishi and home to thousands of workers and their families.

Rise and Development

In 1890, Mitsubishi bought the island and began extracting undersea coal. The company built massive concrete apartment complexes to house workers. By 1959, over 5,000 people lived on the tiny island, creating an extremely dense community with schools, shops, and a hospital.

Forced Labor

During World War II, Koreans and Chinese were brought to Hashima to work in brutal conditions. Many suffered from dangerous work, malnutrition, and abuse. These stories remain a controversial part of Japanâ€™s wartime history.

Decline and Abandonment

In 1974, coal demand fell and the mine closed. The island was quickly abandoned. Buildings were left untouched, and Hashima became a ghost island preserved in time.

Modern Significance

Hashima reopened for tourism in 2009 and became a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2015. However, it remains controversial due to the lack of acknowledgment of forced labor during WWII.
