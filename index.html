Hashima Island

Industry, Abandonment, and the Shadow of History

Introduction

Hashima Island (端島), more commonly known as Gunkanjima ("Battleship Island"), is located 15 kilometers off the coast of Nagasaki. It earned its nickname from its massive sea wall and blocky silhouette, resembling a warship. Originally discovered for its coal deposits in the 19th century, Hashima later became one of the most densely populated areas on Earth before being completely abandoned in 1974.

Japan highlights Hashima as a symbol of rapid industrialization and innovation — but beneath this narrative lies a darker legacy involving forced Korean labor during World War II. As tourists walk among the ruins, a fuller story continues to emerge.

Industrial Rise: Mitsubishi and the Undersea Mines

In 1890, Mitsubishi purchased Hashima and began constructing deep undersea mines to extract coal. Shaft No. 1 reached a depth of over 600 meters by the early 20th century. Reinforced concrete buildings were built starting in 1916 to protect against typhoons, making Hashima home to Japan’s first large-scale reinforced apartment block.

Mitsubishi developed advanced techniques to extend tunnels out beneath the seabed, creating one of the world’s most innovative coal extraction systems at the time. The company housed workers and families on-site, ensuring a full labor force year-round.

“At its peak, more than 5,000 people lived on this small, 6.3-hectare island.” — Japan National Tourism Organization

Life on the Island: A Concrete Community

Despite its harsh appearance, Hashima provided infrastructure ahead of its time. Schools, a hospital, a cinema, communal baths, rooftop playgrounds, and a Buddhist temple were part of daily life. The island had seven-story concrete apartment blocks and a narrow network of corridors and stairs connecting homes and facilities.

However, space was extremely limited. Children played on rooftops. Food and water had to be ferried from Nagasaki daily. The population density peaked at 83,500 people/km² in 1959 — nine times higher than Tokyo at the time.

Forced Labor During WWII

Between 1939 and 1945, as Japan waged war across Asia, it forcibly conscripted over 800 Korean laborers to Hashima. These individuals were housed separately, often in substandard conditions. They worked 12–15 hour shifts in suffocating tunnels with no ventilation, exposed to toxic gases, cave-ins, and brutal oversight.

According to Yonhap News, survivors describe conditions as “hellish.” Many laborers died due to exhaustion, beatings, or accidents. Pay was docked for food and lodging, making escape or savings impossible.

“We were forced to work underground for more than 12 hours a day… many were injured, and some even died.” — Yonhap News Agency

Post-War Decline and Abandonment

After WWII, Korean and Chinese workers were repatriated (many against their will) without reparations. Coal remained central to Japan's economy until the 1960s, when petroleum took over. Mines began to close, and Mitsubishi officially shut down Hashima’s operations in 1974. All residents were evacuated within weeks, leaving homes, furniture, and tools untouched.

The island has remained uninhabited since, slowly crumbling under typhoons and time.

UNESCO World Heritage and Controversy

In 2015, Hashima was designated a UNESCO World Heritage site as part of Japan’s “Sites of Japan’s Meiji Industrial Revolution.” However, international observers — especially South Korea — criticized the nomination for glossing over the forced labor history.

Japan agreed to acknowledge that “some people were brought against their will and forced to work under harsh conditions,” but critics argue that signage and tours remain vague or euphemistic.

Timeline of Hashima Island

1810

Coal discovered on Hashima by local fishermen.

1890

Mitsubishi purchases island and opens first mine shaft.

1916

Japan’s first large reinforced concrete apartment building constructed.

1939–1945

Over 800 Koreans forcibly conscripted for labor during WWII.

1959

Population peaks at 5,267 — world’s highest density.

1974

Mine closes. Island abandoned and sealed off.

2009

Island reopens for tourism with limited access.

2015

UNESCO World Heritage designation and controversy over labor history.

2017

Survivors demand accurate memorialization (Yonhap).

Hashima in Popular Culture

Hashima has fascinated filmmakers and artists worldwide. It was prominently featured in the 2012 James Bond film Skyfall as villain Raoul Silva’s lair — though the scenes were filmed on a replica set. The island also appears in documentaries, video games, and horror films, often symbolizing dystopia or abandonment.
